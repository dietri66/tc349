The Morris Worm

Robert Tappan Morris Jr.

Robert Tappan Morris Jr., a graduate of Harvard and Cornell, was responsible for what is known as one of the first computer worms. While a Cornell grad student, he wrote a piece of code to see how big the internet was. He released the code on November 2, 1988, on an MIT computer to diguise where the attack came from.

The Worm

The worm took advantage of three vulnerabilities in BSD UNIX and Sun systems. The worm was known as one of the first pieces of malware to target multiple computing platforms. The worm gained access and spread through processes such as sendmail, rsh, and fingerd.

The Impact

  • Infected nearly 6,000 computers
  • Estimated between $100,000 and $10 million dollars lost to lack of Internet access
  • Costs to remove the worm were between $200-$53,000
  • US Department of Defense unplugged their Internet to prevent the worm from accesssing their network.

The Punishment

Robert Morris was sentenced to 3 years probation and was required to pay a $10,050 fine. He was also subjected to 400 hours of community service. Robert was one of the first people to be charged under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act.
